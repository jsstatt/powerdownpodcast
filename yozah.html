Phonetics variation:

Within the VCV structure the non-fricative consonants usually become palatalized. Outside of this structure they will often fall more towards a standard pronunciation.

Syllable structure:

Nouns most commonly use CVCVC structure while verbs mostly use VCVC. Article vowels are mostly only seen in articles however both phonemes can exist next to each other in other words. Nouns can only end with a vowel. The minimal structure that is allowed is CV for articles, CV for nouns and VC for verb.  Consonant cluster cannot exceed 2 phonemes without requiring a vowel in between.

Suprasegmental Features:

Tone: Questions are formed using falling tone while statements are made with a medium tone. Aggression can often be expressed with low tone while joy can be expressed with a higher tone.

Stress: The stress of a word usually falls on the nucleus of the syllable so in the common VCV the stress is on the C.

Noun Classes, Definite and Indefinite Articles:

Noun cases are broken up into two categories. The first category is natural. This is any living thing or something that is naturally occurring. The second category is non-natural which is something man-made or otherwise created and not naturally occurring. Articles are either definite or indefinite but must match the noun class of the noun that follows. The natural noun class uses front vowels while the non-natural class uses back vowels. You can only match front vowel articles with nouns that end with front vowels and back vowel articles with nouns that end with back vowels. An article is always needed before a noun

Grammatical Cases:

Yozah follows a rigid word order for different tenses. Grammatical cases are used in order to specify the word’s grammatical function.

Nominative Nouns:

The nominative case indicates that the word is the subject of the sentence. A noun can be identified as being in the nominative case if it does not have any added affixes.

Examples:

Nominative Verbs:

Words that end in yur ­or yer are verbs. This ending on verbs indicate that the verb is in its “to” form [upjaryur = to see]. Verbs only mark grammatical case when a pronoun is attached. If a pronoun is used as the subject of the verb, that pronoun must be attached to the front as a prefix. Additionally, the yur or yer must be declined at the end even though there are not suffixes for this case. Other grammatical cases will use suffixes so make sure to decline the ending.

Adjectives are used to modify the noun by adding more descriptive value. The adjective can be place before the noun that it describes. Adjectives are denotated with the ending -jo however the ending vowel must be changed to match the noun class of the noun that fallows it. The vowel ending will always match the noun class ending not the article class ending. Adjectives can be stacked one after another to create more descriptive meanings.

Syntax

Word Order, Tense, and Aspect:

Tense in Yozah changes with word order. Present tense, like you have seen earlier, is constructed in subject object verb order or SVO. Past tense uses the word order SOV and future tense uses the word order VSO. The easy way to remember this is that when talking about the past everything goes before the verb and when talking about the future everything goes after the verb. Remember that the indirect object always goes before the direct object in any tense. 

Yozah also uses imperfect and perfect aspects to describe ongoing habitual activities and completed actions. Along with the word order the particle uj is added before the verb to indicate if the action is perfect. No particle is needed to indicate that an action is imperfect.

Commands

Commands are very common in Yozah speaking. While they may seem rude in other languages or cultures a command in Yozah is the politest way of requesting an action. They are constructed by using the perfective particle uj before the verb in a present tense statement. While you can still attach pronouns to this verb the original unconjugated ending must remain. This turns the statement into a command.

Examples:

Uj ojuyer – Eat!

Uj umchatyur-mar - answer me!

Uj umazakyur se ya pezha – Play with the dog!

Pragmatics

Speech Acts

In the culture of many Yozah speakers, direct forms of communication are considered to be more polite then indirect. Most Yozah speakers are very blunt with some factions and clans even practicing absolute honesty. This is when speakers never lie during a conversation and generally saying exactly what they are thinking without a filter. It is because of this cultural practice that it is often considered rude to use indirect speech acts as a way to buffer or mask the intent of a speech act.

Orthography

Writing System:

Yozah’s writing system is an abugida, meaning that the vowels are marked on the consonants. Each character has a space in the center dedicated to the vowel that proceeds it. The vowels are marked as either a circle or square filled in or hollow. The squares indicate that the vowel is a non-natural noun class vowel while the circle indicates the natural noun class vowel. If the circle or square if filled in it indicates the article ending is a definite article. If it is not filled in the vowel is represents an indefinite article. Vowels can exist on their own as long as they come before a consonant and do not proceed a consonant. Consonants can exist on their own as long as there is no vowel after it. Palatalization is marked as distanced characters which are usually the character of its base sounds flipped. Words are strung together with a continuous line that only ends at the end of a sentence. Sentences are normally read vertically however horizontal reading is still used and understood. Negations are marked as special characters that are added to the beginning of words they are applied too. Negations always use this character and are never spelled with the characters that make up its sounds (See phonetics for full orthographic list)

History

History of Yozah Writing System:

The history of Yozah language can be broken up into 3 stages: Ancient Yozah, Traditional Yozah and Modern Yozah.

Ancient Yozah:

Ancient Yozah was often written on carvable sandstone and clay tiles. Writers would use small knives known as sosudju to engrave each character into the material. sosudju comes from the word sosu meaning sword. The affix dj when added to sosu translates to little sword. With the invention of writing utensils, the words had remand causing any utensil used to write script to be known as a little sword. This is why the word of pencil and knife are the same word in modern Yozah. Ancient Yozah was purely consonantal meaning that the language only represented consonants in the writing system. Additionally, the writing system did not distinguish between palatalization of consonants. The vowels as well as the consonant palatalization had to be inferred based on the context. Many words that exist in modern Yozah come directly from words from ancient Yozah. While this stage in Yonah’s development is called ancient script there are still hundreds of years prior to these systems that is still undiscovered.

Traditional Yozah:

Traditional Yozah is the script that exists between ancient and the modern system as the transitional period. This stage in the script began to mark vowels. Just like modern Yozah script, vowels were added to the center of the consonant to represent that the vowel that follows the consonant. Vowels in this script only showed if they were are natural or non-natural and did not yet distinguish between the definite and indefinite when used in an article. Traditional script still did not mark palatalization. Writing utensils similar to pencils were used to write this script on sheets of paper. Paper was often reserved for higher ranking members of a clan because of its rarity.

Culture

Yabʲuch

Yozah is the language of the Yabʲuch people. The Yabʲuch are a group of nomadic tech-worshiping people who live in the sprawling rocky deserts of a post-apocalyptic earth. Little is known about the Yabʲuch culture. What is known is that the speakers of Yozah are strange and mysterious and hold technology in high regard. They are seemingly human although no one has ever seen a Yabʲuch person without their iconic garb which conceals their entire body. They speak through a synthesizer voice modulator which makes their voice sound robotic, further hiding their voice. Some fear that the Yabʲuch are anything but human.

 

Because of the obsession that the Yabʲuch culture has with technology, The Yozah language reflect this obsession through the use of noun classes. Since they emphasize technology and man-made creations, their language distinguishes between natural and non-natural nouns. Additionally, the Yabʲuch culture emphasizes people and personal relationships, which is why pronouns are allowed to affix with verbs.

Names:

In Yozah there are no distinctions between masculine and feminine names. All name can be used for any gender. There is however a distinction between youth names and adult names. Youth names are the names that children use until they have been pronounced and adult. A youth name is usually a combination of parts of the parent’s name(s) as well as the ending -ron which means child of.  Once a child reaches adulthood, they able to take on a new name of their choosing. Their clan will help in providing a list of names for the new adult to pick from however it is still welcome to pick something different. Sometimes new adults will take the name of one of their parents as a sign or respect or as a way to carry on a family name. This is most often practiced with royalty. This new name is the adult name and will become their first name while their youth name then becomes their middle name. A person’s last name is the clan or group they are a member of. Adult names will often times have something to do with what the person is good at, what they are known for or what they did to complete their trial to become an adult.
